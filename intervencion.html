Clara de la Lama

Intervención (2018)

Written and Directed by Clara de la Lama

Acting Ansemble: Argeniz Aldrete, Michel de León

Production team: Marily Suam (technical director), Heber Israel Medina (light board and music operator), Michell de León (sound designer), Pedro Salamanca (poster designer)

The problem presented in Intervención comes from the tension between free will and the social as well as natural forces that influence our decision making and judgement. For me, the sects that were common during the 60s and 80s in the United States embody the essence of this tension. Many of them met their end in the suicide of their members, influenced to an extent by a powerful, charismatic (perhaps megalomanic) leader but always as a result of a personal conviction that it was the best thing they could do. Social, philosophical and natural determinism intend to provide a theoretical framework that can englobe all the factors that determine our actions yet fail due to their absolutist nature: human behaviour is significantly too complex to be reduced to social structure, ideology or genetics. The father of one a Young man who comitted suicide as part of the collective suicide undertook by the sect Heaven's Gate made the claim that the event where he lost his son was not the suicide of 39 people but the murder of 38 and the suicide of one -Applewhite, the cult leader-. Studying these very particular events I got stuck with a question: can suicide be the product of a completely willingly desision, or is it always social, or always the product of persuasion, or always a desperate measure? Or, for that matter, can any action we undertake be completely autonomous?

Intervención, a play for two actors written in Spanish, is and exploration of the concepts of free will and determinism, and the human desire to control and exert influence over other people's lives. The structure and dialogues are meant to put the audience in a state of uneasiness; we witness and understand the vulnerability of the less privileged character, while we, like him, are slowly driven into the more privileged one's cosmovision, and his ideas, which at first seem almost completely irrational, start to seem sensible. We all are vulnerable to persuasion, and we all think that our principles, ideas and desires are stable, firm and reconstituted, rather than dynamic and shapeless. The truth is, under the right circumstances, we could all be persuaded to do something that we currently consider unthinkable, let alone doable. In the play, we witness the progression of this sort of cult constituted by two people, into a final act of loyalty, congruence and dignity. Two friends who decide to drop out of their philosophy degree to start a life that feels more significant and achieving to them, embark in a search for transcendence, meaning and, although one more than the other, a means of living.

Less interested in finding a job, the economically privileged one spends most of his time in the ideation of a “big act”, the act through which they will prove their worth, not only for the sake of them two, but to prove the exitance of free will to all of humanity. While he becomes increasingly involved in the logistics and theory behind this, he tries to share his ideas with his friend, who is busy and stressed about earning a living. However, his charisma, emotional support and general understanding slowly capture his friend's attention, who becomes increasingly obsessed with the idea of free will. His mindset then evolves through a trajectory that is both gory and realistic, but, most importantly, utterly relatable.

The way the play was presented is as follows: the actors had freedom to move and walk among the seats, and there were four seats per row; a set of 2 on the left side and another set of 2 on the right side of the hallway. the space must be big enough for the actors to have free mobility between the seats.

Every pair of seats was distributed in space in a way that assimilated a car waiting in the traffic lights of a busy road in Mexico City. Some seats were alone to include individual drivers in the simile. The street vendor (less privileged) character continuingly approached the “driver” of each car offering his products, services, or just asking for spare change

The less privileged character has similar actions in each scene: he sells newspapers and other products in the traffic lights. The audience assumes the role, due to the setup of their seats, of car drivers who are either stuck in traffic or waiting for the lights to go green. The character is trying to sell them his products and they don't; this way, the audience and their indifference are added up to the set of factors that shape his way of thinking, and, eventually, make him change his initial convictions in favour of his friend's project, since his lack of an income, and failure as a street seller, make him abandon hope in his own plans and instead follow the other's ideas. The influence of the audience's indifference in the outcome of events is exposed when, in the play's final scene, the less privileged character announces he is about to commit suicide in the middle of the street and invites the drivers who are witnessing the public act to stop him, that is, to intervene. The name of the play, thus, refers to a non-action that becomes a factor for action, that is, the audience's lack of participation. If a member of the audience decided to intervene in the play by “stopping” the suicide, this intervention would have been influenced by the name of the play, perpetuating the self-fulfilling prophecy cycle that take place in the play, when the privileged character makes things happen by talking to his friend about the likeliness of them happening. Theory and philosophy then prove to be not only a provided explanation of events or a record of observed correlations, but a determinant of events; no longer descriptive but normative.

The official poster design shows the two friends facing each other. The image shows them in the moment of the “act of freedom”, or the performance – corresponding to the last scene -, where they expose their motives to the car drivers and peasants. They wear a mark made of old newspapers as planned by the privileged character to represent all the interrelated events that lead them to this final action.

Since the play only had two performances and a limited number of seats, marketing was far from a priority. However, we tried be coherent with its concept by keeping the word “theatre” or “play” out or all publicity. We wanted to treat the play as a sort of performance, so that the reality of the final act could shock and include the audience as much as possible into the happenings. The audience is not invited to watch a play, but to intervene an act of rebellion or an act of deep accomplishment. After paying for the ticket, the attendants receive a QR code that when scanned will give them an address on google maps, signaling the place where the play will take place. When entering the room, audience members are asked to turn their phones off “to allow the intervention to begin”.
