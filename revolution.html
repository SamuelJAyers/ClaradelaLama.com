Clara de la Lama

The Revolution Will Not Be Televised (2021)

Directed by Clara de la Lama

Acting Ansemble: Víctor Belmont, Mayte Corona, Angélica Domínguez, Mariana Pacheco, Gabriela Jiménez

Production team: Analúa Fenton (light board and music operator), Aldo Caza (sound designer), Paulina Ramírez (poster designer), Itzel Riqué (technical director), Clara de la Lama (arts director)

The play's name, was taken from a song by Gil Scott-Heron's from the 70's. It was highly politicized for its allusion to the “Black Power” movement, and its words were a reply to a piece by The Last poets which said: “when the revolution comes, some of us will probably catch it on TV. The concept in the play is resignified by being used in the context of the feminist movement. Instead of the anti-racism movement, it refers to a feminist revolution, but one that strictly goes beyond any discourse, any institution and any ideology, even feminist ones. This movement escapes the comprehensible and the limitations of logic, and its engine is purely women's faith in something bigger than individuals or ideologies, coordinated, and, most importantly, so complex and secretive that it cannot be fully explained or registered, for the risk of it getting corrupted. There is very little precisely due to the nature of the revolution: every action taken by the revolutionaries is silent and subtle but powerful, significant and so embedded in daily life that, despite its influence and reach, only those who take part in it can notice it. Going back to Scott Heron's song, our rephrasing of his song's chorus would be something like this: "the revolution will not be televised. It will be daily, invisible, life-changing, but carried by everyday women's stare at each other. It will be a flash mob, so well-choreographed that nobody will be able to stop it, because no one will see it happen".

The story is set in a timeless and spaceless reality, which was a challenge when directing it. It happens in a town that seems old but the characters speak, act and dress like people do in the present, an overlap in temporality and space that symbolizes the spread of the feminist movement over the years and different cultures and regions. This discrepancy in temporalities was addressed with the concept vintage: the majority of the scenography was made of wood, most props were old objects and it was only the clothes that they wore that referred the audience to contemporaneity. The idea was to convey the sense of a struggle that is always and everywhere in movement, that lacks a predetermined shape and that is dynamic, liquid, and endless. A question that is never solved because every time an answer is found, the formulation of the question changes.

Since most of the play consisted in actions rather than dialogue, conveying the ideas we wanted to transmit required a big effort and a lot of creativity. The actual revolution consisted on small, hidden actions, on passing objects from one woman to another, on moving signs, on old objects left in strategic spots of public places. The logistics of such secretive actions, however, and never revealed, we only know that they are necessary steps towards a main goal: the growth of the movement through the recruitment of new members, who will in turn be expected to continue the cycle. This took the shape of a sort of choreography of daily actions, with a little twist of secrecy, intensity and intentionality. To achieve this, we undertook a series of improvisation exercises where the actors, who each had a mundane action and a revolutionary action to carry out simultaneously, where indicated to change their spot every 10 seconds. Therefore, one of the girls would be sweeping the floor at a park while writing a message with the leaves she collected, and the other could be speaking to her mother on the phone while moving a black bag with valuable objects from one corner of the park to the other. The exercises we did to achieve this duality were fun, constructive, and effective, and the result was a very organic choreography of daily actions occasionally intervened by subtle and small revolutionary actions.

Another challenge that was solved through a similar method is the scene that was set in a churchyard. Here, the aim was to create a contrast between the religious context of the wedding ceremony that had just taken place inside the church – and as the scene progresses moves into the churchyard space – and the subversive succession of the revolutionary militants, which were mixed in with the wedding attendants. The revolutionary routine had to progressively merge in with the church bustle in a disruptive yet natural way to allow for the mystery of the actions' purpose to be kept and at the same time make a statement about the nature of the struggle. The scene also relied greatly on the soundtrack, since this was the main technical tool with which the tension between the two realities – or cosmovisions- was built. The space of the churchyard with the church's front on the back constituted the religious symbolism and the cultural charge of Catholicism, of which the impact in society's systems of oppression is determinant for the tension in this scene. It is a war of symbols; the symbol of marriage, of a woman wearing white, of the social contract that promotes women's role according to the catholic standards. The religious empire is omniscient, always present in speech and in the narrative that the rituals carry. The sound of the bells announcing mass is a constant reminder of that prevailing structure of power that filtrates even through the senses. They ring loudly throughout the whole play, but it is in this scene that they become confrontive, aggressive, impossible to ignore: it announces mass, marriage, dominance, oppression, tradition. To counteract this micro oppression, thus, the militants had to play the symbolic game as well. They rebelled against those norms by finding each others' eyes in the middle of the crowd, by passing small antique tools to each other. Some of the look exchanged are between militants, some are from a militant to a member of the crowd, recruiting her. The audience then adequate to the protagonist's perspective: they can slowly begin to understand the logic behind the sequence, the rhythm, the coordination. The militants integrate into the events of the wedding to spoil it, they collect the rice thrown at the bride and groom in one big container, they change the white flowers that decorate the way for black ones, they get tangled in the wedding necklace, but to get close they camouflage with those invited to the wedding, they laugh, they chat.

The photo featured in the official poster featured the three friends walking aligned, trying to make their way through a crowd of people. Since the production took place during COVID-19, we held a zoom version of the event for those who preferred to take such safety measures. There were two live performances and each was streamed live for the zoom audience.
